<h1 id="prediction---regression-analysis">Prediction - Regression Analysis</h1>
<h3 id="description"><strong>Description</strong></h3>
<p>In data mining, <strong>Prediction</strong> is the process of identifying patterns in historical data to forecast future numerical outcomes. While classification focuses on “Which category?”, prediction focuses on “How much?”.<br>
<strong>Regression Analysis</strong> is the primary statistical methodology used for this task. It builds a mathematical relationship between known variables to predict a continuous, ordered value.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="what-is-regression-analysis">1. What is Regression Analysis?</h2>
<p>Regression is a way to model the relationship between a <strong>Dependent Variable</strong> (the target result) and one or more <strong>Independent Variables</strong> (the input factors).</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dependent Variable (<span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Y</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Y</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68333em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right: 0.22222em;">Y</span></span></span></span></span>):</strong> The value you are trying to predict (e.g., Temperature, Price).</li>
<li><strong>Independent Variable (<span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>X</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">X</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68333em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right: 0.07847em;">X</span></span></span></span></span>):</strong> The variables that influence the target (e.g., Humidity, Location).</li>
</ul>
<hr>

<p><strong>Important Note:</strong> Despite having “Regression” in its name, Logistic Regression is used for <strong>Classification</strong>. It maps any real-valued number into a value between 0 and 1 using the Sigmoid function, predicting probabilities for discrete classes.</p>

<h2 id="how-the-model-learns-ordinary-least-squares-ols">3. How the Model Learns: Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)</h2>
<p>The most common way to train a regression model is <strong>Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)</strong>.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Residuals:</strong> For every data point, the model calculates the vertical distance (error) between the actual point and the predicted line.</li>
<li><strong>Squaring:</strong> It squares these distances (to remove negative values and punish large errors).</li>
<li><strong>Minimization:</strong> The algorithm mathematically finds the line where the <strong>Sum of Squared Errors (SSE)</strong> is the lowest possible.</li>
</ol>
<hr>
<h2 id="evaluation-metrics-measuring-closeness">4. Evaluation Metrics: Measuring “Closeness”</h2>
<p>Since we aren’t predicting “Right/Wrong” labels, we measure how “close” our predicted numbers are to the actual numbers.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mean Absolute Error (MAE):</strong> The average magnitude of errors (ignores direction).</li>
<li><strong>Mean Squared Error (MSE):</strong> The average of squared errors. It is highly sensitive to outliers.</li>
<li><strong>Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):</strong> The square root of MSE. It is the most popular metric as it is in the same units as the target variable (<span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>Y</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Y</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68333em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right: 0.22222em;">Y</span></span></span></span></span>).</li>

<h2 id="challenges-in-regression">5. Challenges in Regression</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Multicollinearity:</strong> When your “independent” variables are actually dependent on each other (e.g., trying to predict house price using both “Total Square Feet” and “Number of Rooms,” as they usually increase together).</li>
<li><strong>Outliers:</strong> A single extreme value (like a mansion in a middle-class neighborhood) can “pull” the regression line away from the majority of data, ruining the model.</li>
<li><strong>Heteroscedasticity:</strong> When the “noise” or error in our data isn’t constant across all values of <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>X</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">X</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68333em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right: 0.07847em;">X</span></span></span></span></span>.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="real-world-applications">6. Real-World Applications</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Sales Forecasting:</strong> Predicting how many units of a product will sell next month based on past trends.</li>
<li><strong>Weather Prediction:</strong> Estimating the exact degrees of temperature or centimeters of rainfall.</li>
<li><strong>Economic Trends:</strong> Predicting the inflation rate or GDP growth for a country.</li>
<li><strong>Health Metrics:</strong> Predicting a person’s life expectancy based on lifestyle habits and genetics.</li>
</ol>
